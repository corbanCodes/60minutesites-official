Insurance Website Checklist: 15 Must-Have Elements

8 min read

Most insurance agents know they need a website, but knowing what makes one effective is different. An insurance website that generates leads and builds trust requires specific elements that generic business sites often miss.

This checklist covers the 15 elements that separate insurance websites that work from those that just exist. Use it to audit your current site or plan a new one.

The Complete Insurance Website Checklist

Work through each item and note where your current website stands. Even implementing half of these will put you ahead of most competitors in your market.

1. Clear Value Proposition Above the Fold

Within seconds of landing on your homepage, visitors should understand exactly what you offer and why they should choose your agency. Are you an independent agent with access to multiple carriers? Do you specialize in certain coverage types? Serve a specific community?

The area visitors see before scrolling should answer: What insurance do you sell? Where do you operate? What makes you different? How do they get a quote?

2. Visible Phone Number on Every Page

Insurance shoppers often want to talk to someone, especially for complex coverage needs or when they have questions. Your phone number should be visible in the header of every page, and on mobile devices, it should be tap-to-call enabled. Do not make potential clients hunt for how to reach you.

3. Quote Request Forms That Convert

Your quote forms are the conversion point of your website. They should be prominent, easy to find, and simple enough to complete. Essential fields include:

  • Name and contact information
  • Type of insurance needed
  • Current coverage status
  • Brief description of needs or questions

Avoid requesting detailed information like driving records or policy numbers at this stage. Capture the lead first, then gather details during the consultation.

4. Dedicated Pages for Each Insurance Product

Create separate pages for auto, home, life, business, and any specialty lines you offer. Each page should explain the coverage type in plain language, discuss who needs it, and include relevant calls-to-action. Product-specific pages help with SEO and demonstrate expertise in each area.

5. Professional Credentials Display

Display your insurance licenses, professional designations, and continuing education commitments. Include your license numbers as required by state regulations. Credentials like CPCU, CIC, or AAI signal expertise and professionalism to prospects.

6. Carrier Relationships (For Independent Agents)

If you are an independent agent, your ability to shop multiple carriers is a key advantage. Display the carriers you represent. This demonstrates your ability to find the best coverage and pricing for each client rather than being limited to one company's products.

7. Mobile-Responsive Design

Over half of your visitors will view your site on phones or tablets. If your website is difficult to navigate on mobile devices, you are losing leads to competitors with responsive sites. Test your site on multiple devices and fix any issues with navigation, readability, or form functionality.

8. Service Area Definition

Be clear about where you serve clients. List the cities, counties, or states where you are licensed and actively writing business. This helps visitors quickly confirm you can help them and improves your local search visibility.

9. About Page with Personal Connection

Insurance is a relationship business. Clients want to know who they are trusting with their coverage decisions. Your about page should include your background, experience, philosophy, and what makes your agency different. Include photos and make it personal.

10. Fast Loading Speed

Slow websites frustrate visitors and hurt search rankings. Test your site speed using tools like Google PageSpeed Insights. Optimize images, minimize code, and use quality hosting. Aim for pages that load in under three seconds.

11. SSL Certificate (HTTPS)

Your website must use HTTPS, indicated by the padlock icon in browsers. This is a basic security requirement for any site that collects personal information. Most hosting providers include SSL certificates at no extra cost. Sites without HTTPS may display security warnings that scare away visitors.

12. Clear Calls-to-Action

Every page should have a clear next step for visitors. Use specific language like "Get Your Free Quote" or "Start Saving Today" rather than generic "Contact Us" buttons. Make buttons visually prominent and place them where visitors naturally look.

13. Trust Signals and Social Proof

Include elements that build trust with prospects:

  • Google reviews or other rating platforms
  • Industry association memberships
  • Years in business
  • Community involvement mentions
  • Any awards or recognition received

14. Privacy Policy

If you collect personal information through your website, you need a privacy policy. This is both a legal requirement in many jurisdictions and a trust signal for visitors. Explain what information you collect, how it is used, and how it is protected.

15. Google Business Profile Integration

While not technically part of your website, your Google Business Profile works together with your site for local visibility. Ensure your profile is claimed, verified, and optimized. Keep information consistent between your website and Google profile.

Prioritizing Your Improvements

If your current website is missing multiple elements from this checklist, prioritize based on impact:

Start here: Phone number visibility, quote request forms, mobile responsiveness. These directly affect your ability to receive inquiries.

Next priority: Product pages, credentials display, service area definition. These build the trust needed to convert visitors into leads.

Then address: Page speed, about page content, SSL certificate. These improve visitor experience and search visibility.

Finally: Trust signals, privacy policy, Google profile optimization. These refine your conversion rate once the foundation is solid.

Your Website Audit Action Items

Review your current insurance website against this checklist. Note which elements are present, which need improvement, and which are missing entirely. Prioritize fixes based on their impact on lead generation and work through them systematically.

An insurance website that includes all 15 elements will outperform the majority of competitor sites that treat their online presence as an afterthought. The investment in getting these details right pays dividends every time a prospect finds your site and decides to request a quote.
