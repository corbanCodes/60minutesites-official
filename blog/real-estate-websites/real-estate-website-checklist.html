Real Estate Website Checklist: Must-Have Elements for Agents

9 min read

Most real estate agents know they need a website, but understanding what makes one truly effective is a different matter. A real estate website that generates leads and builds trust requires specific elements that generic business templates often miss.

This checklist covers the essential elements that separate real estate websites that work from those that just exist. Use it to audit your current site or plan a new one.

The Complete Real Estate Website Checklist

Work through each item and note where your current website stands. Even implementing half of these will put you ahead of most competing agents in your market.

1. Clear Value Proposition Above the Fold

Within seconds of landing on your homepage, visitors should understand who you are, what areas you serve, and what makes you different. The above-the-fold area, what visitors see before scrolling, should answer: What type of real estate do you specialize in? Where do you work? Why should someone choose you?

Avoid generic statements like "Your Real Estate Expert." Instead, be specific: "Helping First-Time Buyers in Portland Find Their Perfect Home" or "Luxury Home Specialist in the Bay Area Since 2008."

2. Professional Agent Photo

Real estate is a personal business. Clients want to see who they will be working with. Include a high-quality, professional headshot prominently on your homepage and about page. The photo should be current, well-lit, and reflect your professional image. Avoid casual snapshots or outdated photos from years ago.

3. Visible Phone Number on Every Page

Real estate clients often need immediate assistance. Your phone number should be visible in the header of every page. On mobile devices, enable tap-to-call functionality. Consider displaying your text messaging number as well, as many clients prefer texting over calling.

4. Property Search or Listings Display

Your website should provide access to property listings in some form. This might be full IDX integration allowing MLS searches, featured listings from your current inventory, a sold properties gallery showcasing your track record, or links to your listings on portal sites.

The level of integration depends on your budget and business model, but some listing content is expected on any real estate website.

5. Service Area Definition

Be explicit about where you work. List the specific neighborhoods, cities, or regions you serve. If you specialize in certain areas, highlight them. Create dedicated pages for your primary service areas with neighborhood-specific information. Visitors should quickly confirm whether you work in their target location.

6. Buyer Resources Section

Provide valuable content for potential buyers researching their purchase. Include information about the homebuying process, what to expect during a transaction, mortgage basics and pre-approval guidance, home inspection overview, and first-time buyer specific resources.

This content establishes your expertise while keeping buyers engaged with your site.

7. Seller Resources Section

Similarly, create content that serves potential sellers. Cover topics like preparing a home for sale, pricing strategies and market analysis, the listing process from start to closing, staging and presentation tips, and what to expect during negotiations.

Consider offering a home valuation tool or CMA request form to capture seller leads.

8. Detailed About Page

Your about page should go beyond a basic bio. Include your real estate background and experience, credentials and designations, areas of specialization, personal background and interests, community involvement, and your approach to client service.

Write in first person and let your personality come through while maintaining professionalism.

9. Credentials and Trust Indicators

Display your professional credentials prominently. Include your license information, professional designations (CRS, ABR, GRI, etc.), brokerage affiliation, years of experience, transaction volume or sales statistics, and awards and recognition.

These elements answer legitimate questions that clients have about your qualifications.

10. Neighborhood or Community Guides

Demonstrate local expertise through detailed neighborhood content. Create pages covering housing styles and price ranges, schools and education quality, amenities and lifestyle factors, market trends and statistics, and what makes each area unique.

These pages serve relocating buyers and boost local SEO visibility.

11. Mobile-Responsive Design

More than half of real estate searches happen on mobile devices. Your website must function flawlessly on phones and tablets. Test navigation, forms, property listings, and contact features on actual mobile devices. Ensure buttons are appropriately sized for touch and content is readable without zooming.

12. Lead Capture Forms

Include strategic lead capture opportunities throughout your site. Consider home valuation request forms, property search registration, buyer consultation requests, seller listing consultations, newsletter signup for market updates, and downloadable guides requiring contact information.

Keep forms concise while capturing enough information to follow up effectively.

13. Clear Calls-to-Action

Every page should have a clear next step for visitors. Use specific, action-oriented language like "Schedule a Buyer Consultation," "Get Your Home's Value," or "Request a Private Showing." Avoid generic "Contact Me" buttons that do not indicate what happens next.

14. Fast Loading Speed

Real estate websites with many property photos can load slowly if not optimized. Compress images before uploading. Use efficient hosting. Test your site speed using Google PageSpeed Insights. Mobile users especially will not wait for slow-loading pages.

15. SSL Certificate (HTTPS)

Your website must use HTTPS, indicated by the padlock icon in browsers. This is essential for security, visitor trust, and search engine rankings. Most hosting providers include SSL certificates. If your site still shows "Not Secure," address this immediately.

16. Google Business Profile Integration

While separate from your website, your Google Business Profile should be optimized and linked to your site. Keep information current, add photos regularly, encourage and respond to reviews, and post updates about your business. Many local searches show Google Business results before website listings.

17. Contact Page with Multiple Options

Provide multiple ways to reach you on a dedicated contact page. Include phone, email, text, office address if applicable, and a contact form. State your typical response time and preferred contact methods. Make it easy for prospects to communicate in whatever way they prefer.

18. Current Content and Listings

Nothing undermines credibility like outdated information. Ensure featured listings are current, not sold months ago. Update sold properties regularly. If you have a blog, post regularly or remove date stamps. Review all content quarterly for accuracy.

Prioritizing Your Improvements

If your current website is missing multiple elements from this checklist, prioritize based on impact:

Start here: Phone number visibility, professional photo, mobile responsiveness, contact form functionality. These directly affect your ability to receive and respond to inquiries.

Next priority: Property listings or search, service area definition, about page content. These build the foundation visitors need to trust you.

Then address: Buyer and seller resources, neighborhood guides, credentials display. These establish expertise and provide SEO value.

Finally: Lead capture optimization, refined CTAs, speed improvements. These optimize conversion once your foundation is solid.

Your Website Audit Action Items

Review your current real estate website against this checklist. Note which elements are present, which need improvement, and which are missing entirely. Create an action plan based on the prioritization above and work through improvements systematically.

A real estate website that includes all these elements will outperform the majority of competing agent sites that treat their online presence as an afterthought. The investment in getting these details right pays dividends with every lead your website generates.
