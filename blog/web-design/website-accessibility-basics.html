Website Accessibility Basics for Small Business

5 min read

Imagine walking into a store, only to find that the door is locked for half the people who want to enter. Frustrating, right? That’s what inaccessible websites can feel like for individuals with disabilities. Making your website accessible isn’t just a legal requirement; it’s a way to welcome everyone into your virtual space. By understanding and implementing website accessibility, you not only comply with regulations but also build a stronger connection with your audience. This is especially important in diverse neighborhoods, where inclusivity can set you apart from competitors.

What is Website Accessibility?

Website accessibility means designing your site so that everyone, including people with disabilities, can use it effectively. This involves ensuring content is perceivable, operable, understandable, and robust. Here are key aspects to consider:

  • Text alternatives for images: Every image should have an alt text description. For example, instead of just saying 'dog,' a better description would be 'a brown Labrador retriever playing fetch in a park.' This helps screen readers convey the image's purpose to visually impaired users.
  • Keyboard navigation: Ensure that all interactive elements on your website can be accessed using a keyboard alone. This is crucial for users who cannot use a mouse. Provide clear tab orders and focus indicators to enhance the user experience.
  • Descriptive links: Avoid using vague phrases like 'click here.' Instead, use descriptive links that provide context, such as 'Read more about our community initiatives.' This makes it easier for all users, especially those using assistive technology, to understand where the link will take them.
  • Color contrast: Ensure there is sufficient contrast between your text and background colors. For example, dark text on a light background is generally more readable. Use tools to check contrast ratios and adjust accordingly.
  • Readable font sizes: Use a minimum font size of 16px and ensure that users can easily adjust the text size without breaking the website layout. This helps those with visual impairments read content comfortably.

Why Accessibility Matters for Small Businesses

Prioritizing website accessibility can have several advantages for your small business, especially in vibrant communities like Lincoln Park or Wicker Park. Here’s why it matters:

  • Broader audience reach: By making your website accessible, you open your doors to a significant segment of the population, including individuals with disabilities. In a city like Chicago, where diversity is celebrated, embracing inclusivity can enhance your customer base.
  • Improved SEO: Accessible websites often rank better on search engines. Search engines favor clear, well-structured content that is easy to navigate, which coincidentally aligns with accessibility best practices.
  • Positive brand image: Showcasing a commitment to inclusivity can enhance your brand's reputation. Customers appreciate companies that care about all members of the community, which can lead to increased customer loyalty.
  • Compliance with legal standards: With the rise in lawsuits regarding digital accessibility, ensuring your website meets legal standards can protect you from potential legal issues. Understanding and adhering to the Americans with Disabilities Act (ADA) can save you time and resources in the long run.

Simple Steps to Improve Your Website Accessibility

You don’t need to overhaul your entire site to make it accessible. Here are straightforward steps to get started:

  • Use alt text for all images: Take the time to craft meaningful descriptions for your images. This not only aids those using screen readers but also improves your SEO.
  • Ensure keyboard navigation: Test your website to ensure all elements are accessible via keyboard navigation. This includes forms, menus, and interactive features. You can simulate this by using the 'Tab' key to navigate through your site.
  • Check color contrast: Utilize online tools or browser extensions to assess the color contrast on your site. Aim for a contrast ratio of at least 4.5:1 for normal text and 3:1 for large text. Adjust colors as necessary to enhance readability.
  • Provide transcripts for audio and video content: Offering transcripts ensures that users who are deaf or hard of hearing can access your content. Moreover, this practice benefits all users by providing additional context.
  • Regularly test your site using accessibility tools: Utilize tools like WAVE or Axe to evaluate your site’s accessibility. Regular testing can help identify areas for improvement and keep your site compliant as you make updates.

Resources for Learning More

To deepen your understanding of website accessibility, consider these valuable resources:

  • Web Content Accessibility Guidelines (WCAG): Familiarize yourself with these guidelines, which provide comprehensive information on best practices for improving accessibility.
  • WAVE Accessibility Evaluation Tool: This user-friendly tool allows you to evaluate the accessibility of your web pages and provides actionable feedback.
  • Color Contrast Analyzer: A handy tool for checking text visibility against backgrounds, helping you maintain proper contrast standards.
  • Online courses on accessibility best practices: Platforms like Coursera and Udemy offer courses that can enhance your understanding of web accessibility. Learning from industry experts can equip you with the knowledge to make informed decisions.
